# Dimension Guide — Brand Voice Extraction

Detailed instructions for analyzing each of the 9 voice dimensions. For 
each dimension: what to look for, what good analysis looks like, and 
common mistakes that produce weak results.

---

## Dimension 1 — Reader Addressing

**What you're measuring:** Which grammatical person the author uses when 
speaking to the reader, and how consistently.

**What to look for:**
- Count instances of "ti" (you, singular familiar), "Vi" (you, formal 
  capitalized), "vi" (you, plural lowercase), and constructions that avoid 
  direct address entirely
- Note the very first sentence of each post — addressing choice is often 
  most deliberate here
- Look for "mi" constructions where the author includes themselves 
  ("mi koji se bavimo ovim", "mi koji vodimo računa o koži")
- Notice when addressing SHIFTS within a single post (e.g., starts formal, 
  relaxes to familiar mid-post)

**What good analysis looks like:**
> "Ti" form dominates at ~80%. Author uses "ti" from the first sentence 
> and maintains it throughout. Examples: "Ako primetiš da ti koža..." 
> (Post 3), "Kada sledeći put naneseš..." (Post 7). "Vi" appears only in 
> formal contexts like booking CTAs: "Zakažite konsultaciju" — suggesting 
> a deliberate shift to signal a business transaction.

**Common mistakes:**
- Counting "vi" (plural) as formal "Vi" — they are different forms
- Missing addressing entirely because the author uses impersonal 
  constructions ("Kada koža postane suva..." instead of "Kada ti koža...")
- Ignoring shifts — a profile that says "uses ti" when the author actually 
  uses "Vi" in CTAs misses an important nuance

## Dimension 2 — Post Openings

**What you're measuring:** The structural pattern the author uses to begin 
each post — this is one of the strongest voice fingerprints.

**What to look for:**
- Read only the first paragraph of each post
- Classify each into one of: direct question to reader, personal 
  experience anecdote, statistic or research citation, myth debunking 
  ("Mnogi misle da...", "Često čujem da..."), direct problem entry, 
  concept definition, or client quote framing ("Često me klijentkinje 
  pitaju...")
- Note exact opening words — authors develop habits ("Da li ste ikada...", 
  "Znate onaj osećaj kada...", "Pre nekoliko nedelja...")

**What good analysis looks like:**
> 7 of 10 posts open with a direct question or client-quote frame. Examples: 
> "Da li ste se ikada zapitali zašto vam koža svetluca ujutru ali izgleda 
> umorno uveče?" (Post 1) — direct question. "Klijentkinja me je nedavno 
> pitala..." (Post 4) — client quote frame. The remaining 3 open with 
> personal practice anecdote. No post begins with a statistic or definition. 
> Pattern: author draws reader into a shared experience before explaining.

**Common mistakes:**
- Classifying "Mnogi ne znaju da..." as a question — it's a myth frame
- Missing that "Nedavno mi je došla..." is an anecdote opener, not a CTA
- Reporting "varied openings" without identifying the dominant pattern

## Dimension 3 — Sentence Length and Rhythm

**What you're measuring:** The structural rhythm of the prose — the 
pattern that makes one writer sound different from another at a mechanical 
level.

**What to look for:**
- Count words per sentence across 3-4 representative paragraphs from 
  different posts (not just one post)
- Calculate average
- Find the longest typical sentence (90th percentile — ignore outliers 
  like lists formatted as sentences)
- Look for deliberate short sentences used for emphasis — often 1 sentence 
  standing alone in a paragraph
- Look for dashes (—), colons, and ellipses (...) — these extend sentence 
  rhythm in ways that word count misses
- Note if sentences vary or if they're uniformly medium-length (the AI tell)

**What good analysis looks like:**
> Average sentence: 12 words. Most sentences fall between 8-18 words. 
> Author uses very short sentences (3-6 words) for emphasis, always 
> standalone: "To je to." (Post 2), "Nema prečice." (Post 6). Maximum 
> typical sentence ~28 words. Uses dashes frequently to extend thought 
> without starting a new sentence: "Koža reaguje na sve — stres, san, 
> vodu, godišnje doba." Rhythm alternates: medium → medium → short punch → 
> medium. This creates momentum that uniform AI prose lacks.

**Common mistakes:**
- Measuring only one post and claiming it's representative
- Ignoring punctuation rhythm (dashes, colons) and only counting words
- Not noting the SHORT sentence pattern — this is often the author's 
  most distinctive habit

## Dimension 4 — Paragraphs

**What you're measuring:** How the author chunks information — this 
signals the intended reading experience (scan vs. deep read).

**What to look for:**
- Count sentences per paragraph across multiple posts
- Count words per paragraph
- Note if single-sentence paragraphs appear, and in what contexts 
  (emphasis? transition? conclusion?)
- Find the densest paragraph — what's the maximum the author will write 
  before breaking?
- Note if paragraph length varies by section (e.g., short intro paragraphs, 
  longer explanatory ones)

**What good analysis looks like:**
> Average: 2.3 sentences, ~35 words per paragraph. Frequently uses 
> 1-sentence paragraphs for transitions and conclusions: these appear 
> before and after main argument blocks. Never exceeds 4 sentences in a 
> single paragraph — long explanations are broken into multiple 2-sentence 
> blocks. This creates a scan-friendly structure: reader can skip and 
> still follow the argument.

**Common mistakes:**
- Counting list items as paragraphs — they're not
- Missing that paragraph length is intentional, not arbitrary
- Reporting only average without noting the maximum and the single-sentence 
  pattern

## Dimension 5 — Lexical Habits

**What you're measuring:** The specific vocabulary fingerprint — words 
and phrases that appear consistently and are NOT just industry vocabulary.

**What to look for:**

*Recurring signature words:* These appear in multiple posts and are not 
just topic-driven. They reveal the author's conceptual vocabulary. 
Example: a cosmetologist might write "rutina" (not "režim"), "negu" 
(not "tretman"), "koža voli" (as if skin has preferences).

*Translation choices:* Does the author translate English beauty/wellness 
terms or use them directly? This is especially diagnostic in Serbian — 
"hidratacija" vs "moisturizing", "njega" vs "skincare", "zaštita od sunca" 
vs "SPF" or "sunscreen".

*Technical term handling:* When they introduce a technical term 
(hyaluronic acid, ceramides, retinol), do they define it immediately? 
Use a lay equivalent? Assume the reader knows?

*What's consistently absent:* This is harder to spot but critical. List 
words that a generic writer would use but this author doesn't. If they 
never use superlatives ("savršen", "idealan", "revolucionaran"), that's 
a strong signal.

**What good analysis looks like:**
> Signature words: "rituali nege" (appears in 6/10 posts), "klijentkinje" 
> (never "klijenti" — always feminine form), "koža zaslužuje" (skin 
> deserves), "godišnje doba" (seasons, not "period"). 
> Translation: consistently avoids English terms — writes "hidratantna 
> krema" never "moisturizer", "retinol" is the one exception (no Serbian 
> equivalent used). Technical terms always get a lay translation in 
> parentheses on first use: "ceramidi (masne kiseline koje čuvaju zaštitni 
> sloj kože)". 
> Absent: never uses "revolucionaran", "inovativan", "savršen". Never 
> writes "klijent" (masculine) when referring to clients.

**Common mistakes:**
- Listing only high-frequency words that are just topic vocabulary 
  ("koža", "tretman" — every cosmetologist uses these)
- Missing the absent words — this requires deliberately looking for what 
  ISN'T there
- Not noting the translation policy — this is highly diagnostic for 
  Serbian content

## Dimension 6 — Emotional Register

**What you're measuring:** The emotional stance the author takes toward 
the reader and the subject.

**What to look for:**
- Look for empathy signals: does the author acknowledge the reader's 
  frustration, confusion, or concern before offering advice?
- Look for authority signals: does the author lead with data, or with 
  personal experience?
- Look for conversational signals: anecdotes, dialogue, "zamislite..." 
  ("imagine..."), direct address
- Look for educator signals: patient step-by-step explanations, "krenimo 
  od početka" ("let's start from the beginning")
- Look for mentor signals: wisdom from experience, "naučila sam tokom 
  godina da..." ("I've learned over the years that...")
- Note when the register SHIFTS — many authors have a warm opening but 
  become more clinical mid-post

**What good analysis looks like:**
> Primary register: Mentor — shares wisdom from practice without positioning 
> as an expert lecturing from above. Evidence: uses "naučila sam" and 
> "u mojoj praksi" frequently, presents personal failures alongside 
> successes ("Sama sam prošla kroz..."). Secondary register: Educator — 
> explanations are structured and patient, never assumes prior knowledge. 
> NOT empathetic in the "I feel your pain" sense — empathy comes through 
> by sharing personal experience rather than validating feelings directly.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using "warm and professional" as a register description — this is a 
  personality description, not a register analysis
- Missing the secondary register — most authors have two
- Confusing empathetic language ("razumem") with the empathetic register 
  (which requires consistent pattern, not just occasional phrases)

## Dimension 7 — Authority Building

**What you're measuring:** How the author establishes credibility without 
self-promotion.

**What to look for:**
- Specific experience references: "u 15 godina rada", "tokom karijere" vs 
  generic "u dugogodišnjem iskustvu"
- Client story patterns: "Nedavno mi je došla klijentkinja koja..." — 
  note how stories are introduced and what they prove
- Research citations: do they cite studies? How? ("Istraživanje iz 2022..." 
  vs "nauka kaže..." vs nothing)
- Limit acknowledgment: does the author say "ne znam" or "zavisi" — 
  this is a strong credibility signal when done right
- Before/after patterns: do they reference client outcomes? With or 
  without numbers?

**What good analysis looks like:**
> Authority built primarily through client story pattern: "Prošle nedelje 
> mi je došla klijentkinja sa..." appears in 5/10 posts, always followed 
> by diagnosis + outcome. Research cited occasionally and informally: 
> "studije pokazuju" without specific citation — suggests awareness of 
> research without academic register. Strong limit-acknowledger: "ne 
> postoji odgovor koji važi za sve" appears in 3 posts. Never uses 
> specific numbers for client outcomes ("povećali smo za 40%") — keeps 
> outcomes qualitative ("koža se transformisala"). Years of experience 
> never stated directly — implied through client story volume.

**Common mistakes:**
- Treating self-deprecation as lack of authority — it's often the 
  opposite in this register
- Missing limit acknowledgment as an authority signal
- Not noting HOW stories are framed (the formula: situation → what I 
  noticed → what we did → outcome)

## Dimension 8 — CTA and Calls to Action

**What you're measuring:** How (and whether) the author transitions from 
content to action at the end of posts.

**What to look for:**
- Read only the last 2-3 paragraphs of each post
- Classify the ending: direct CTA (specific action), soft invitation 
  ("ako imate pitanja..."), open question to reader, reflective close 
  (no action), educational close ("nadam se da sada razumete...")
- Note exact CTA phrasing — this is very consistent in authors with a 
  strong voice
- Note whether CTAs are in familiar ("zakažite" — you plural/formal) or 
  familiar ("zakaži" — you singular informal) form — should match the 
  addressing in the post

**What good analysis looks like:**
> 6/10 posts end with soft invitation: "Ako imate pitanja ili želite da 
> razgovaramo o vašoj koži, tu sam." (Post 1, 4, 7). 3/10 end with open 
> reader question: "A vi — koji je vaš ritual koji ne preskačete?" 
> (Post 3, 6, 9). 1/10 ends reflectively with no CTA. No post uses a 
> direct hard CTA ("Zakažite odmah"). CTA voice matches post addressing: 
> "tu sam" not "tu smo" — maintains the 1:1 relationship tone.

**Common mistakes:**
- Missing that the CTA form (Vi vs ti) must match the post's addressing 
  pattern — inconsistency is itself a finding
- Reporting "ends with CTA" without noting the softness/directness level
- Not counting the "no CTA" posts — they're part of the pattern

## Dimension 9 — Forbidden List (What They Never Do)

**What you're measuring:** The negative space — consistent absences that 
are as defining as what's present.

**This is the hardest dimension.** You're looking for things that are 
NOT in the text despite being common in comparable content.

**Systematic checklist — look for each of these across all posts:**

- [ ] Emojis anywhere in the text (not just CTAs)
- [ ] ALL CAPS for emphasis
- [ ] Mid-sentence bolding (as opposed to bolding headings or key terms 
      at start of bullet)
- [ ] Exclamation points (count them — most strong writers use 0-1 per post)
- [ ] Product brand names mentioned by name
- [ ] Concrete result promises ("garantujem", "sigurno ćete", "100%")
- [ ] Clickbait headline patterns ("Ovo će vas šokirati", "Tajna koja...")
- [ ] Generic transition phrases ("kao što smo rekli", "da zaključimo")
- [ ] Rhetorical questions as section headers
- [ ] Lists of 10+ items (vs shorter, curated lists)
- [ ] Personal photos/anecdotes about non-professional life
- [ ] Negative competitor references

**What good analysis looks like:**
> Forbidden: emojis (zero across 10 posts), exclamation points (2 total, 
> both in quoted client speech), product brand names (always writes 
> "hidratantna krema" not "Cerave"), concrete promises ("nema garancija" 
> — author explicitly avoids them twice), clickbait patterns (all headlines 
> are descriptive, none use "šokantno" or "tajna"). Also absent: never 
> writes lists longer than 5 items, never uses "kao što smo već rekli" 
> (AI transition tell).

**Common mistakes:**
- Listing only 2-3 items — minimum is 5, and 8+ is better
- Listing generic items like "doesn't use complex language" — this is 
  a style choice, not an absence pattern
- Missing that absence of brand names is a strong signal (competitor 
  avoidance vs brand neutrality)
