# Serbian Language Patterns — Brand Voice Reference

Reference for analyzing and writing authentic Serbian content. Covers AI 
tells in Serbian prose, authentic voice markers, the ti/Vi system, and 
beauty/wellness industry register conventions.

---

## Part 1 — AI Tells in Serbian Text

These patterns appear systematically in AI-generated Serbian content. 
Their presence suggests the analyzed text may itself be AI-generated 
(and therefore not a valid brand voice source). Their ABSENCE in client 
texts is a sign of authentic human writing.

### Transition phrase AI tells

Serbian AI content overuses these connective phrases:

- "Kao što smo već rekli..." / "Kao što smo napomenuli..."
- "Važno je napomenuti da..."
- "Treba istaći da..."
- "U zaključku možemo reći..."
- "Na kraju krajeva..."
- "Sve u svemu..."
- "U današnje vreme..."
- "U modernom svetu..."
- "Nije tajna da..."

**How to use this:** If client blog posts use these phrases frequently, 
flag this — either the posts are AI-generated, or the client has an 
unusually formal/academic style. Confirm with user before treating as 
authentic voice.

### Structural AI tells

- **Perfect three-point structure:** AI defaults to exactly 3 reasons, 
  3 tips, 3 steps. Human writers use 2, 4, 5, 7 — imperfect numbers.
- **Symmetric parallel lists:** Every bullet the same length and structure. 
  Human lists vary.
- **Opening sentence that restates the headline:** "Hidratacija kože je 
  veoma važna tema o kojoj ćemo danas govoriti." Human writers enter the 
  content directly.
- **Meta-commentary about the article:** "U ovom tekstu ćemo vam objasniti 
  sve što trebate znati o..." Human writers just explain.

### Vocabulary AI tells in Serbian

- Overuse of "izuzetno" (extremely) as an intensifier
- "Stoga" as a formal connector where "zato" would be natural
- "Ukoliko" instead of "ako" in informal contexts
- "Naime" at the start of explanatory sentences (very common in Serbian 
  AI output)
- "Dakle" used as a sentence opener (not wrong, but overused)
- "Pomenuti" / "navedeni" when referring back to something (very formal)
- "Od suštinskog je značaja" instead of "važno je"
- Consistent use of passive voice: "može se videti", "treba napomenuti" 
  instead of active voice

## Part 2 — Authentic Human Voice Markers in Serbian

What distinguishes a real person's writing from well-prompted AI output.

### Incomplete thoughts and mid-thought pivots

Real writers leave sentences that are structurally "imperfect":
- Starting with "E, sad..." or "Elem..." (colloquial pivots)
- Dashes that add a thought the writer had while typing: "Koža — naročito 
  mešovita — posebno reaguje na..."
- Parenthetical asides that interrupt the main point: "Ova krema (kupila 
  sam je slučajno, nije bila ni u planu) promenila je moju rutinu."

### Personal time markers

Real writers anchor stories to specific, slightly imprecise time:
- "Pre nekih mesec dana..." (not "Pre mesec dana")
- "Negde početkom jeseni..."
- "Prošle nedelje" (not "U prošloj nedelji" — the latter sounds translated)
- "Tek ovih dana sam shvatila..."

### Colloquial Serbian constructions AI avoids

These appear in authentic informal-to-medium register writing:
- "E, to je cela filozofija." (standalone sentence after a point)
- "I to je to." / "Prosto toliko."
- "Nije slučajno što..." (at start of paragraph)
- "Recimo ovako..." (introducing a simplified explanation)
- "Da budem iskrena..." (personal disclosure marker)
- "Ono što retko ko kaže je..." (setting up an insider perspective)
- "Mnoge od vas su me pitale..." (inclusive, community-building)

### How real experts hedge differently from AI

AI hedges with disclaimers. Real experts hedge with experience:

**AI hedging:** "Treba napomenuti da rezultati mogu da variraju od osobe 
do osobe i da je uvek preporučljivo konsultovati stručnjaka."

**Real expert hedging:** "Ne postoji jedan odgovor — u 15 godina rada 
videla sam da ista krema na jednoj klijentkinja svetluca, a na drugoj 
izazove reakciju." (Specific + from practice + implicitly positions them 
as the expert who knows the difference)

## Part 3 — The Ti/Vi Addressing System

Critical for Serbian content — the choice signals the relationship between 
writer and reader.

### "Ti" (singular familiar, lowercase)

Signals: peer-to-peer, community, informal expertise, wellness/lifestyle 
content aimed at women, personal blog register

Grammatical markers:
- Verb forms: "primetiš", "naneseš", "pitaš se", "znaš"
- Possessives: "tvoja koža", "tvoj ritual", "tvoje pitanje"
- Direct address: "ti sigurno znaš", "zar ne?"

When to use: dominant in beauty, wellness, lifestyle, parenting, personal 
development content on Serbian market. Default register for Instagram 
captions and blog posts targeting women 25-45.

### "Vi" (formal, capital V)

Signals: professional services, medical, legal, financial, or contexts 
where age gap or professional hierarchy matters

Grammatical markers:
- Verb forms: "primetite", "nanesite", "pitate se", "znate"
- Possessives: "Vaša koža", "Vaš ritual"

When to use: Booking CTAs and service pages even in otherwise "ti" content 
(signals transition to business context). Medical clinics. B2B contexts.

### "vi" (plural lowercase)

Signals: addressing a group, community mode, or ambiguous register

Common in: "vi koji ste pratili moje savete", "vi koje se suočavate sa..."

### Mixed addressing (diagnostic signal)

Many writers unconsciously shift between ti and Vi. This is worth noting:
- Consistent shift in CTAs (ti content → Vi CTA): deliberate professional 
  distance at the conversion moment
- Inconsistent shift mid-post: usually a sign of uncertain register or 
  edited content
- Shift in direct quotes: natural (the person being quoted has their own 
  register)

## Part 4 — Beauty and Wellness Register in Serbian

Industry-specific language patterns for salons, cosmetology, aesthetics, 
and wellness brands on the Serbian market.

### Translation conventions in Serbian beauty content

**Translate these — Serbian equivalents exist and are commonly used:**
- moisturizer → hidratantna krema / hidratant
- cleanser → sredstvo za čišćenje / gel za čišćenje
- toner → tonik
- serum → serum (loan word, no translation needed)
- sunscreen / SPF → zaštita od sunca / faktor zaštite
- anti-aging → anti-age (semi-adopted), or "against premature aging" = 
  "protiv prevremenog starenja"
- skin barrier → zaštitni sloj kože / kožna barijera
- glow / glowing → sjaj / koža koja sija (not "glo")

**Terms used directly (no Serbian equivalent in common use):**
- retinol (not "retinol" — the term IS Serbian in usage)
- peptidi (peptides — already Serbianized)
- AHA, BHA, PHAkiseline (used as abbreviations, explained on first use)
- ceramidi (ceramides — Serbianized)

**Never use in Serbian content:**
- "skincare" (write "nega kože" or "rutina nege")
- "self-care" (write "briga o sebi")
- "beauty routine" (write "rutina lepote" or just "rutina")
- "glow-up" (no direct translation; describe the concept)

### Common register markers for beauty/wellness in Serbian

**Informal, personal-brand register (blog, Instagram):**
- "klijentkinja" (feminine noun — cosmetologists typically refer to 
  female clients with feminine form)
- "ritual nege" not "rutina nege" (ritual implies intentionality)
- "koža voli..." (skin loves... — personification is common)
- "Slušajte svoju kožu" (Listen to your skin)
- "Moja preporuka" not "Naša preporuka" (personal accountability)

**More formal/clinical register (medical aesthetics, dermatology):**
- "pacijentkinja" (patient, feminine) not "klijentkinja"
- "preporučujem" not "predlažem" (recommend not suggest — stronger)
- Passive constructions appear more: "može se primeniti", "preporučuje se"
- Latin terms used directly without translation

### Seasonal and cultural anchors (Serbian market)

These appear in authentic Serbian beauty content and signal local writing:
- "jesenska nega" / "prolećna rutina" (seasonal routines are a major 
  content theme)
- "zagrejemo li se za..." (colloquial for "warming up to", used as 
  seasonal transition)
- Post-summer skin repair is a major content topic (August-September)
- "pred zimu" (before winter) as a motivation frame
- Religious/name day references appear occasionally in more personal 
  content ("za slavu", "pred praznike")

AI-generated content tends to use generic seasonal references ("when 
summer comes", "during cold months") rather than these specific Serbian 
seasonal anchors.

## Part 5 — Punctuation Conventions in Serbian

Different from English conventions — important for authentic output.

### Quotation marks

Serbian uses „lower-upper" style: „ovako" not "ovako"
- Opening: „ (double low)
- Closing: " (double high)
- In quotes within quotes: ‚inner' (single low-high)

AI frequently uses English-style quotation marks in Serbian text. Real 
Serbian writers use the correct „..." style (though informal blog writing 
sometimes uses English style — note which the client uses).

### Dashes

Serbian uses the em dash (—) without spaces in some style guides, 
with spaces in others. Note which style the client uses and replicate it.

Common uses in Serbian prose:
- Appositive: "Moja klijentkinja — žena od 45 godina — koristila je..."
- Amplification: "Koža reaguje na sve — stres, san, vodu."
- Abrupt end of thought (rarer): "Htela sam da kažem —"

### Ellipsis

Used more freely in Serbian informal writing than in English. Often 
signals trailing thought or invitation: "Ako se pitate zašto je to 
tako..." (a rhetorical invitation, not an incomplete sentence).

Note how often the client uses ellipsis — it's a stylistic signal.
